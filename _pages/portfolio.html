LLM-Powered Agent-Based Retail Market Simulation

ICBC BRDC

Context: This exploratory prototype studied whether LLM-based agents could complement traditional agent-based models in simulating retail-investor behavior under news shocks. The focus was on controlled scenarios where false news, rumor-like signals, panic, or hype could affect heterogeneous financial-user archetypes.

Keywords: LLM agents, agent-based modeling, retail investor behavior, news sentiment, information diffusion, temporal interaction graphs, cascade analysis, community detection, centrality, behavioral evaluation.

Core problem: Explore whether heterogeneous LLM agents can simulate how different investor archetypes respond to sentiment shocks, especially false-news-driven panic or hype, and whether the resulting behavioral patterns can be analyzed without treating raw LLM outputs as simulation truth.

My contribution: Supported scenario design, behavioral-role abstraction, prompt/data templates, and structured decision-output design. I also proposed a lightweight temporal graph diagnostic layer, representing simulated investors, archetypes, or news exposures through nodes and edges, and used graph-style summaries such as adoption curves, cascade size, community-level clustering, early-adopter centrality, and news-sentiment propagation patterns.

Research relevance: This project helped me become familiar with LLM-agent simulation workflows, scenario-controlled prompting, structured output templates, evaluation-oriented summaries, and temporal graph diagnostics for interpreting agent behavior.

Financial Regulatory Compliance Knowledge Graph

KPMG LightHouse

Context: This implementation-focused project transformed fragmented regulatory documents, internal rules, and compliance clauses into a structured knowledge base for compliance search, training, and rule relationship analysis in a commercial banking scenario.

Keywords: knowledge graph, ontology engineering, Chinese financial-legal NLP, Neo4j, Cypher, NER, BERT classification, TF-IDF, label-quality control, LLM-assisted annotation.

Core problem: Convert heterogeneous regulatory text into auditable entities, relations, and labels, while adapting the labeling strategy to different label frequencies and ambiguity levels.

My contribution: Supported ontology refinement, including entity categories, relation definitions, label granularity, and compliance-oriented schema choices. I assisted regulatory text preprocessing, term-frequency and TF-IDF-style vocabulary inspection, labeling, and label-quality checks. The workflow combined manual annotation and review for rare or edge-case labels, ML-assisted approaches for lower-resource categories, and BERT-style classification for more frequent document or passage categories. I also experimented with Chinese LLM-assisted label augmentation for ambiguous categories and helped validate structured entities and relationships in Neo4j.

Research relevance: This project made me more familiar with an end-to-end Chinese NLP pipeline, including corpus cleaning, domain lexicon customization, entity recognition, text classification, label-quality control, ontology design, graph loading, and query validation. It also strengthened my interest in schemas and ontologies as practical tools for making constraints explicit.

AML Detection for Certificate of Deposit Transactions

ICBC BRDC

Context: This project supported anti-money-laundering risk detection for certificate of deposit transactions. Early non-graph features derived from blacklist or rule-based screening could over-flag harmless users with frequent but ordinary deposit and withdrawal behavior. The harder signals were often relational and temporal, involving repeated counterparties, short-range indirect relationships, abnormal frequency bursts, and time-windowed behavioral changes. However, the available infrastructure was a governed relational-database environment rather than a dedicated graph database.

Keywords: AML, certificate of deposit transactions, temporal features, network-derived features, relational databases, recursive SQL, local graph structure, low-hop neighborhoods, anomaly detection, explainability, constrained data infrastructure.

Core problem: Represent suspicious transaction behavior as auditable temporal and local-network features when full graph infrastructure is unavailable and compliance review requires interpretable feature logic.

My contribution: Designed and tested temporal, behavioral, and local network-derived feature logic through relational-database workflows. The feature work included transaction-frequency bursts, repeated counterparties, short-range transaction chains, indirect account relationships, time-windowed behavioral shifts, and threshold-sensitive risk summaries. I also supported modeling workflows that combined unsupervised anomaly detection with supervised classification, and helped prepare explainable case summaries for review.

Research relevance: This project connected graph-inspired reasoning with constrained data infrastructure. Building graph-style features inside relational tables gave me a more concrete understanding of how graph structure appears locally through neighborhoods, walks, co-counterparty patterns, node/edge-level signals, local aggregation, and temporal changes. It also helped me become familiar with AML feature engineering, explainability requirements, threshold sensitivity, and production-facing data constraints.

Privacy-Preserving Synthetic Data Generation for Cross-Industry Marketing

ICBC BRDC

Context: This project explored synthetic sequential transaction data as a practical alternative to raw-data sharing in a bank-partner marketing collaboration. The intended downstream direction was a privacy-preserving collaboration scenario, such as federated-learning-based coupon targeting with a food-delivery platform partner, but my work focused on synthetic data preparation and validation before downstream model training was completed.

Keywords: synthetic data, transaction sequences, privacy-preserving collaboration, TimeGAN-style generation, distributional validation, utility/privacy trade-off, federated-learning scenario, coupon targeting.

Core problem: Support cross-industry collaboration where useful transaction patterns are needed for analysis or targeting, but raw sensitive data, personal identifiers, and detailed behavioral records cannot be directly shared.

My contribution: Assisted user-level, merchant-level, and transaction-sequence feature preparation, including partner-related transaction labels, demographic segments, customer activity patterns, and temporal indicators suitable for sequence modeling. I helped prepare sequential datasets and configure input pipelines for TimeGAN-style generation, and contributed to validation thinking through distributional similarity checks, utility-oriented summaries, and privacy/governance-aware documentation.

Research relevance: This project helped me become familiar with synthetic-data workflows, sequential representation, distributional validation, utility/privacy trade-offs, and governance-aware evaluation. It also clarified the boundary between data-generation work and downstream model-training claims.

LLM Data Infrastructure, Schema-Aware SQL Generation, RAG, and Complaint Analysis

ICBC BRDC

Context: This work focused on practical LLM infrastructure for banking analytics. The goal was not to build a general-purpose chatbot, but to make internal data access and complaint analysis more efficient under schema, governance, and reliability constraints.

Keywords: schema-aware SQL generation, RAG, database schema grounding, structured extraction, complaint analysis, banking analytics, prompt/data templates, auditable outputs.

Core problem: Ground LLM workflows in database schemas and standardized output formats so that generated SQL and complaint-analysis results are more usable, auditable, and suitable for downstream analytics than free-form model responses.

My contribution: Curated and structured schema-aware SQL-generation examples, including database descriptions, query templates, and instruction-style training samples. I helped integrate schema information into a retrieval-augmented workflow so SQL generation could be grounded in correct table and field definitions. For complaint analysis, I designed structured extraction templates that separated emotional expression, candidate cause, evidence span, and standardized output fields. I also contributed technical documentation and proposal writing for internal innovation projects.

Research relevance: This project helped me become familiar with schema-grounded LLM pipelines, RAG-style data access, structured output design, evidence-span extraction, and reliability constraints in banking analytics.

Cold-Start Financial Product Recommendation for a "What's Your MBTI?" Mobile Bank Campaign

ICBC BRDC

Context: This project supported a youth-oriented mobile banking campaign that used a shareable "what's your MBTI?" style interaction as a user-engagement and new-user-activation hook. The user-facing interaction could recommend financial products after a playful label input, but the recommendation logic relied mainly on user metadata, available financial/behavioral data, and product attributes, with self-reported MBTI-style labels used only as weak campaign signals.

Keywords: cold start, recommender systems, hybrid recommendation, collaborative filtering, content-based recommendation, metadata-based user similarity, segmentation, weak signals, feature engineering, evaluation, user activation.

Core problem: Improve recommendation under sparse user behavior, limited investment history, long-tail product constraints, and practical marketing rules, without treating playful campaign labels as validated personality science.

My contribution: Supported feature design, segmentation logic, metadata-based user similarity, label-to-product affinity rules, evaluation-oriented data preparation, and analytical summaries. I helped translate campaign labels into coarse behavioral indicators such as risk preference, liquidity preference, novelty seeking, product-complexity tolerance, and likely financial goals, while keeping stronger weight on available metadata and financial-behavioral signals.

Research relevance: This project helped me become familiar with recommender-system principles and patterns, including cold-start handling, the limits of pure collaborative filtering under sparse behavior, hybrid recommendation, content/product metadata features, segmentation, weak-signal use, and practical evaluation.
